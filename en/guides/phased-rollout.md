## Why Not Roll Out to Everyone at Once

The most common reason new systems (processes, tools, knowledge bases) fail in teams is **not** bad design — it's **launching to everyone simultaneously**. When everyone has to change habits at once, everyone resists at once.

The core idea of phased rollout:

- Pick **one layer / one scenario** as pilot
- Let it run a full closed loop
- Use **quantifiable exit conditions** to decide expansion

---

## Criteria for Choosing a Pilot

Picking the right pilot is half the battle. A good pilot meets all three:

1. **Most painful now**: the worse the current state, the more visible the marginal gain
2. **Short loop**: one full input → process → feedback cycle completes within 1–2 weeks
3. **Clear downstream signal**: improvements are perceptible downstream, creating word-of-mouth

Anti-patterns:
- Pilot on a scenario that's "already fine" → no one cares
- Pilot on a loop that takes a quarter → no data to prove value
- Pilot where improvement is invisible downstream → expansion stalls

---

## Four Constraints for Phase 1 Scope

A pilot-scope document should state four categories:

| Constraint | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| ✅ Mandatory coverage | Which directories/processes must follow the new system |
| ⏸️ Template ready, not enforced | Where templates exist for voluntary use |
| ❌ Explicitly excluded | What features are **definitely not** enabled during pilot |
| ⚠️ Owner | Who is accountable (without this, the project floats) |

The "explicitly excluded" list is often more important than the "included" list — it protects the pilot team from scope creep.

---

## Exit Conditions for Expansion

When does the pilot count as success? Write it down **before** starting:

- **Quantitative**: core-process execution/compliance rate rises from baseline A to target B
- **Closed loops**: at least N complete loops (root cause + prevention)
- **Subjective**: pilot team owner confirms friction is acceptable

Also write **stop/rollback conditions**:

- Team actively bypasses the system for 2 consecutive weeks → friction too high, step back
- Maintenance cost > decision-improvement value → ROI unjustified

Pilots without exit/rollback conditions become zombie projects — neither expanding nor stopping.

---

## Reference Cadence

A typical 4-week pilot:

- **Week 1**: confirm owner, choose scenario, freeze baseline metrics
- **Week 2**: first full closed loop runs through
- **Week 3**: link with upstream/downstream metrics
- **Week 4**: retrospective, decide expand / adjust / stop per exit conditions

If the owner isn't named by end of Week 1, **do not start Week 2** — an ownerless project will float.

---

## When to Fully Roll Out

Only consider expanding to the next scenario when all three hold:

1. Pilot's quantitative metrics meet exit conditions
2. Pilot team runs on its own (no ongoing push needed)
3. Next scenario's owner is already identified

**Expand one layer at a time**. Expanding two layers means carrying two sets of friction simultaneously.
